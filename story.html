My Internship Search for Summer of 2019

Hi, my name's Ethan Burrell, I'm a sophomore EECS student at UC Berkeley. Like all my peers, I was looking for a learning experience for my 13 week break from school from May to August and initially had great trouble finding a position at first.

I started off the fall semester by attending carrer fairs and networking events where I would speak with recruiters and engineers from many very cool companies. At the end of these conversations (the contents of the conversations usually revolved around my projects) I would hand them my resume or they would ask for my email. I was over the moon when the latter happened, but realized something interesting: no one would email me back.

As finals for Fall 2018 approached and I had only heard (decisisions and interviews/challenges) from a small subset of companies that I had applied to, I knew over the break I had to strategize on how to change the outcome of a simple application.

During my break, I realized that I was just applying for every position I could find online, and maybe I should try and narrow down to companies that I had done relevant projects and research about. Here is where I learned a lot. I do research about the consequqnces of the hot housing climate of the Bay Area and decided to use this special knowledge for new companies to apply to. I found cool real-estate startups like Stessa, WayPoint, and LocateAI (among others), and decided to put my application in for their jobs. Here, the responses to my application went up dramatically. I also noticed if I wrote ~200 characters in the "Other Questions box", I would much more likely get a response. With this knowledge that there were interns needed in the real estate tech sector, I set off on interviews and my Spring Semester was off to an infinitely better start than the Fall one.

I had always heard that interviewing is an art, and over the winter break I chose to read aricles and books on how to do this better. By talking to so many firms in such a short period of time, I was able to read recruiters reactions to my responses and fine tuned how my technical inspired interviews should have gone. Even though a lot of these companies maybe did not have the resources to take on an intern, just having the opportunity to be in a real phone interview helped me learn so much about the interviewing, recruitment, and technical screening processes.

The Graphs

I made some graphs over my winter break so that I could roughly see where I was failing in the interview process. At first it seemed like my initiial applicaiton would get no friction in the Application Tracking System, with some changes to my resume I was able to fix this. Later in the Spring semster, I was able to get more responses to my application, then I realized I needed to work on interviewing skills. This simple visualizer helped me spot some pitfalls in my self and helped me improve and learn as a job-seeker. Here's how the chart works:
The graph shows a flow from the initiial application (company name) to the next step of the application process. As you can see, a lot right to "Denied" (this process usually takes ~1-2 months). Other companies move to other categories, here's how we define them:

  • Coding Challenge: An online assignment that tested some techinal skill: algorithms, math, or system design
  • Phone Screen: A call with a recruiter/engineer, I didn't keep track of how many I had for each company, just if I had one
  • Follow up: If a company sent an email asking for more information or other events (not a call or a challenge)
  • On-site: A meeting where I met a recruiter in person (usually at a companies HQ)
  • Offer: A company offered me a position for the summer!!! Woohoo
  • Withdrawal: I had recieved offers from companies that I prefered, so I withdrew my application from some companies
  • Accepted: Signing the paperwork for the summer!
